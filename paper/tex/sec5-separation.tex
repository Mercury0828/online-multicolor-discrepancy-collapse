\section{The online--offline separation}\label{sec:separation}

The two halves of the analysis, the offline upper bound of \Cref{thm:offline} and the online lower bound of
\Cref{thm:online}, can now be placed side by side. Read together they say that the online and offline values live on
genuinely different scales, and so they give a multicolor analogue of the two-color separation of \cite{AT2025}. In the
two-color setting that separation already shows that an adversary who reveals the columns one at a time forces an
asymptotically larger imbalance than an offline colorer who sees the whole matrix; the statement below records that the
same phenomenon survives, and in fact sharpens, when the palette has any fixed number of colors.

\begin{theorem}[Separation, fixed $k$]\label{thm:separation}
For every fixed $k\ge 2$ and $2\le d\le\Hscale^2/\log\Hscale$,
\[
  \ondisc_k\ =\ \Th(\loglog n)\ \gg\ \offdisc_k\ =\ \Th(\sqrt d)\ =\ o(\loglog n)\qquad\whp.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
There are three things to verify: that the online value sits at $\Th(\loglog n)$, that the offline value sits at
$\Th(\sqrt d)$, and that the sparsity cap forces the second to be of strictly smaller order than the first.

The online value is $\Th(\loglog n)$ by \Cref{thm:online}, which supplies both directions at fixed $k$. The lower bound is
the heart of the matter: no online rule can keep the maximum per-coordinate spread below order $\loglog n$, because the
adversary can always feed columns that drive the rank statistic upward at the doubly logarithmic rate forced by the
self-similar branching of the allocation. The matching upper bound shows that the rank-greedy rule keeps the spread at
$\Oh(\loglog n)$, so the online order is pinned at $\Th(\loglog n)$.

The offline value of a random $d$-regular set system, $k$-colored with a constant number of colors, is $\Th(\sqrt d)$. For
two colors this is the classical discrepancy of a random sparse set system, namely $\Th(\sqrt d)$ from
\cite{BansalMeka2019,EzraLovett2016,HobergRothvoss2019}. Passing from two colors to a fixed number $k=\Th(1)$ of colors
changes the value only by a constant factor that depends on $k$, and not on $n$ or $d$. To see this, fix a balanced
$k$-coloring and look at one row, that is, one coordinate $i$. The $d$ columns that contain $i$ are split among the $k$ color
classes, and the load of color $c$ on row $i$ is the number of those columns assigned color $c$. Under a balanced coloring
each of the $T$ columns is color $c$ with probability $\Th(1/k)$, and a fixed column contains row $i$ with probability $d/n$,
so the load of color $c$ on row $i$ is
\[
  x_{i,c}\ \sim\ \Bin\!\big(\Th(T/k),\,d/n\big),
\]
a sum of roughly $T/k$ independent indicators each of probability $d/n$. Its mean is
$\Th(T/k)\cdot(d/n)=\Th(Td/(nk))=\Th(d/k)$, since the regularity constraint fixes $Td/n=\Th(d)$ column-incidences per row,
and its variance is $\Th(T/k)\cdot(d/n)(1-d/n)=\Th(d/k)$ because $d/n\to0$. Hence each color load has standard deviation
\[
  \sqrt{\Var(x_{i,c})}\ =\ \Th(\sqrt{d/k}).
\]
The per-coordinate spread the offline objective measures is the gap $\max_c x_{i,c}-\min_c x_{i,c}$ between the largest and
smallest of these $k$ color loads. For the upper direction, each $x_{i,c}$ deviates from its common mean $\Th(d/k)$ by at most
$\Oh(\sqrt{(d/k)\log k})=\Oh(\sqrt{d/k})$ for $k=\Th(1)$ with probability $1-1/k^{\Om(1)}$, by the Bennett tail of
\eqref{eq:poisson-tail} applied to the binomial (the small-deviation regime, since the deviation is of order one standard
deviation), so a union bound over the $k=\Th(1)$ colors keeps every load within $\Oh(\sqrt{d/k})$ of the mean and the spread
is $\Oh(\sqrt{d/k})=\Oh(\sqrt d)$. For the lower direction, the loads are nondegenerate: a single color load $x_{i,c}$ has
standard deviation $\Th(\sqrt{d/k})$ and, by the local central limit theorem for the binomial with mean $\Om(1)$ used in
\eqref{eq:poisson-tail}, puts mass $\Om(1)$ both above $\Th(d/k)+\Th(\sqrt{d/k})$ and below $\Th(d/k)-\Th(\sqrt{d/k})$. Since
distinct color loads are asymptotically independent (they read disjoint blocks of column-color assignments), with probability
$\Om(1)$ one color lands at the high end and another at the low end, forcing $\max_c x_{i,c}-\min_c x_{i,c}=\Om(\sqrt{d/k})$.
The spread of a constant number of loads each with deviation $\Th(\sqrt{d/k})$ is therefore $\Th(\sqrt{d/k})=\Th(\sqrt d)$,
since $k=\Th(1)$ enters only through a bounded multiplicative constant. The classical offline algorithm attains this spread up
to constants, so the offline value is $\Th(\sqrt d)$.

It remains to compare the two scales. Under the sparsity cap $d\le\Hscale^2/\log\Hscale$ we may take square roots, and since
$d\mapsto\sqrt d$ is increasing this gives
\[
  \sqrt d\ \le\ \sqrt{\Hscale^2/\log\Hscale}\ =\ \frac{\Hscale}{\sqrt{\log\Hscale}} .
\]
Now $\Hscale=\loglog n\to\infty$ as $n\to\infty$, so $\log\Hscale\to\infty$ and the prefactor $1/\sqrt{\log\Hscale}\to0$.
Therefore $\sqrt d\le\Hscale/\sqrt{\log\Hscale}=o(\Hscale)$, that is $\sqrt d=o(\loglog n)$: the offline value is of strictly
smaller order than $\loglog n$, with the ratio $\offdisc_k/\loglog n=\Oh(1/\sqrt{\log\Hscale})\to0$ quantifying how much
smaller. Therefore $\offdisc_k=\Th(\sqrt d)=o(\loglog n)=o(\ondisc_k)$, which is exactly the displayed chain of the theorem.
\end{proof}

A word on why the offline value here is quoted from the classical random-set-system bounds and not read off from
\Cref{thm:offline}. \Cref{thm:offline} is proved in the growing-palette regime $k\to\infty$, where its hypotheses are in
force, so it does not directly cover a fixed $k$. The two descriptions agree on the overlap of their ranges: the offline
formula $\Psifn(\lam,\log k)$ of \Cref{thm:offline} collapses to $\Th(\sqrt d)$ when $k$ is held constant, because the
$\log k$ argument is then a bounded constant and $\Psifn$ reduces to its Gaussian-spread regime. So the two routes give the
same offline order on the common domain, and we use whichever is licensed by its hypotheses.

For growing $k$ the online value is no longer pinned to a single power of $\loglog n$, and the clean separation above gives
way to a two-sided estimate. Combining \Cref{thm:offline} (through the trivial inequality $\ondisc_k\ge\offdisc_k$, since any
online coloring is in particular an offline one) with the online lower and upper bounds
\Cref{lem:online-lower,lem:online-upper} gives the following bracket.

\begin{corollary}[Growing-$k$ bracket]\label{cor:bracket}
For $C_0\log^5(dk)\le k\le d$ with $d,k=(\log n)^{o(1)}$,
\[
  \max\!\Big\{\,\Psifn(\lam,\log k),\ \frac{\loglog n}{k}\,\Big\}\ \le\ \ondisc_k\ \le\ \loglog n\qquad\whp.
\]
(The floor $\loglog n/k$ alone, from \Cref{lem:online-lower}, needs no lower bound on $k$; the $\Psifn$ floor uses
\Cref{thm:offline} and hence its hypothesis $k\ge C_0\log^5(dk)$.)
\end{corollary}

The two endpoints of this bracket can be far apart, and which of the two floors dominates depends on the size of $k$
relative to $\loglog n$. The lower bound is the maximum of two terms of very different origin. The term $\Psifn(\lam,\log k)$
is the offline floor: it is a price that even an all-seeing colorer must pay, inherited from \Cref{thm:offline}. The term
$\loglog n/k$ is online-specific: it is the cost the doubly logarithmic branching imposes on any column-by-column rule, and
it has no offline counterpart. For small $k$ the online-specific term $\loglog n/k$ is the larger of the two and strictly
exceeds the offline floor $\Psifn$, so online coloring is genuinely worse than offline coloring in that range, which is the
qualitative content of the separation extended past fixed $k$. As $k$ grows the online-specific term decays like $1/k$,
while the offline floor $\Psifn$ grows with its $\log k$ argument, so the two must cross.

We can locate the crossover. Write $L=\log k$ for the second argument of $\Psifn$. In the regime $\lam=\Th(d/k)$ of order one,
the occupancy floor reduces to $\Psifn(\lam,L)=\Th(1+\sqrt{\lam L}+L/\log(e+L/\lam))=\Th(\sqrt{L})=\Th(\sqrt{\log k})$, since
the middle term $\sqrt{\lam L}$ dominates when $\lam=\Th(1)$ and $L=\log k\to\infty$. The online-specific floor is
$\loglog n/k=\Hscale/k$. The two floors are equal when
\[
  \frac{\Hscale}{k}\ \asymp\ \sqrt{\log k},
\]
that is when $k\sqrt{\log k}\asymp\Hscale$, whose solution is $k=\Th\!\big(\Hscale/\sqrt{\log\Hscale}\big)$ up to the doubly
logarithmic correction (taking logs of $k\sqrt{\log k}=\Th(\Hscale)$ gives $\log k=\Th(\log\Hscale)$, and feeding this back
gives $k=\Th(\Hscale/\sqrt{\log\Hscale})$). So the crossover sits at $k\approx\loglog n$ up to a $\sqrt{\log\loglog n}$
factor: below it the online-specific floor $\Hscale/k$ is the larger of the two and dominates, above it the occupancy floor
$\Psifn$ takes over. In between, the bracket leaves a genuine gap between the lower and upper bounds, and pinning the
exact growing-$k$ order inside that gap is the subject of \Cref{sec:open}.

Two consequences are worth stating. First, the separation of \Cref{thm:separation} is exponential in the offline value: the
online value $\Th(\loglog n)$ is exponentially larger than the offline value $\Th(\sqrt d)$ across the whole sparse range
$2\le d\le\Hscale^2/\log\Hscale$, since $\sqrt d$ can be as small as $\Th(1)$ while $\loglog n\to\infty$. Second, the
separation is not an artifact of a single palette size: it persists for every fixed $k\ge2$, with the implied constants
depending on $k$ only through bounded factors, so the online disadvantage is a stable structural feature of the problem and
not a coincidence at one value of $k$.
