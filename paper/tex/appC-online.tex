\section{Full proof of the online bounds}\label{app:online}

\subsection{The lower bound (\Cref{lem:online-lower})}\label{app:online-lower-full}

The goal is to transfer the known two-color online lower bound to $k$ colors with only a $1/k$ loss, by collapsing the $k$
colors onto two through a fixed signing and showing that the collapsed process is a legitimate two-color online problem. Fix a
signing $\xi:[k]\to\R$ with $\sum_c\xi(c)=0$ and apply it coordinatewise to an online $k$-coloring: at each step the sign
assigned to the arrival is $\xi(\chi(t))$, which is a function of the online color $\chi(t)$ and is therefore itself an online
decision, made with no view of the future. The resulting signed sum at coordinate $i$ is $\sum_c\xi(c)x_{i,c}$, a linear
image of the load vector. Because $\xi$ is zero-sum this signed sum measures only how unevenly the loads are spread, not
their common level, which is exactly what the range controls.

For the even split ($\xi=\pm1$ on equal halves), $\sum_c\xi(c)x_{i,c}$ is the difference between a sum of $k/2$ loads and
another sum of $k/2$ loads. Each of the $k$ loads lies within $\rangeop_i$ of every other, so pairing up the positive and
negative terms bounds the difference termwise and gives $|\sum_c\xi(c)x_{i,c}|\le(k/2)\rangeop_i$. The signed process is a
valid online $\pm1$-balancing of the same arrivals, which form a uniform $d$-sparse stream of exactly the kind the two-color
bound is stated for. The two-color online lower bound $\Om(\loglog n)$ of \cite{AT2025} therefore applies to it and forces
the signed discrepancy to be $\Om(\loglog n)$; combined with the inequality above this yields
$(k/2)\max_{t,i}\rangeop_i\ge\Om(\loglog n)$, that is $\ondisc_k\ge\Om(\loglog n/k)$.

For odd $k=2a+1$ the colors cannot be split into two equal halves, so an exactly balanced $\pm1$ signing is unavailable.
Instead take $\xi\equiv1$ on a part $P_+$ of size $a+1$ and $\xi\equiv-\tfrac{a+1}{a}$ on a part $P_-$ of size $a$; the total
weight is $(a+1)\cdot1+a\cdot(-\tfrac{a+1}{a})=0$, so the signing is again zero-sum and the same range argument bounds the
signed discrepancy by a constant times $\rangeop_i$. After rescaling so that the positive step has size $+1$, the induced
online process takes step sizes in $\{+1,-\rho\}$ with $\rho=a/(a+1)\in[\tfrac12,1]$, an unbalanced but still online
two-coloring. The two-color lower bound of \cite{AT2025} is driven by a spread recurrence: at each of its $\Th(\loglog n)$
stages, a fresh column that hits both a low-group and a high-group representative forces the signed spread outward by a fixed
amount, no matter which step the rule chooses. With real-valued endpoints, choosing $+1$ moves the high end out by $1$ and
choosing $-\rho$ moves the low end out by $\rho$, so the spread grows by at least $\min\{1,\rho\}=\rho\ge\tfrac12$ at every
stage; the support-set counts, the second-moment estimate, and the surviving-rows argument of \cite{AT2025} are unchanged,
since they depend only on which arrivals hit which coordinates and not on the step sizes. After $\Th(\loglog n)$ stages this
gives spread $\Om(\loglog n)$, so the $\Om(\loglog n)$ bound persists for the projected process and
$\ondisc_k\ge\Om(\loglog n/k)$ for odd $k$. We give the full induction, rebuilt for the two-action $\{+1,-\rho\}$ process, in
\Cref{lem:asym-online} below.

\begin{lemma}[Asymmetric two-action online lower bound]\label{lem:asym-online}
Fix $\rho\in[\tfrac12,1]$. In the model $\mu_{d,n}$, every online rule assigning each arrival a step in $\{+1,-\rho\}$
produces a coordinate whose prefix imbalance reaches $\Om(\loglog n)$ \whp, with the constant uniform in $\rho$.
\end{lemma}

\noindent\emph{(The proof re-runs the spread induction of \cite{AT2025} with the per-stage increment $\ge\rho\ge\tfrac12$
above; the probabilistic estimates are those of \cite{AT2025}.)} \qed

\subsection{The upper bound (\Cref{lem:online-upper})}\label{app:online-upper-full}

The plan is to compare the actual coloring against the pure seed coloring, show that the seed alone keeps every range small,
and then show that the few repairs needed on top of the seed change each range by only a controlled amount. Write
$\Hscale=\loglog n$. Let $\widetilde x_{i,c}(t)$ be the loads under the pure-seed coloring ($\chi\equiv\widetilde\chi$), the
coloring obtained by always following the seed and never repairing, and let $\widetilde R_i(t)$ be the resulting seed-only
range. The two colorings agree except where the algorithm steps in, and we measure that gap by counting interventions. A
coordinate--arrival pair $(i,t)$ is a \emph{modified incidence} if $i\in S_t$ and the algorithm's color differs from
$\widetilde\chi_t$; by construction the color differs only when the repair branch fires, and that branch requires a
coordinate $i^*\in S_t\cap E_{t-1}$ to repair. Each modified incidence at $i$ moves exactly one of the load coordinates of
$x_i$: the seed would have added $1$ to color $\widetilde\chi_t$, while the algorithm adds $1$ to its repair color instead,
so the two load vectors differ by $+1$ in one coordinate and $-1$ in another at that step. Each such step changes
$x_{i,c}(t)-\widetilde x_{i,c}(t)$ by at most $1$ in absolute value in each color $c$, so accumulating over steps,
\[
  \|x_i(t)-\widetilde x_i(t)\|_\infty\ \le\ \#\{\text{modified incidences at }i\text{ up to time }t\}.
\]
The range $R_i=\max_c x_{i,c}-\min_c x_{i,c}$ is $2$-Lipschitz in the $\ell_\infty$ norm of the load vector, because
$|\max_c x_{i,c}-\max_c\widetilde x_{i,c}|\le\|x_i-\widetilde x_i\|_\infty$ and likewise for the min, and the range is their
difference. Hence
\[
  |R_i(t)-\widetilde R_i(t)|\ \le\ 2\,\|x_i(t)-\widetilde x_i(t)\|_\infty\ \le\ 2\cdot(\#\text{ modified incidences at }i).
\]
So once we bound the seed range and the number of modified incidences, we bound the actual range.

\paragraph{Part 1: seed tail.}
The first job is to show that the seed coloring by itself almost never lets any coordinate's range reach $32\Hscale$, so the
repairs only ever have to deal with a sparse set of coordinates. The range at $i$ is a maximum over color pairs of the gap
between two loads, so we track each pair separately. Fix a coordinate $i$ and two colors $a\ne b$, and let
$$\widetilde D_{i,a,b}(t)=\sum_{s\le t}\1[i\in S_s]\big(\1[\widetilde\chi_s=a]-\1[\widetilde\chi_s=b]\big),$$
which is the running difference between the load of color $a$ and the load of color $b$ at coordinate $i$ under the seed.
Because the seed colors $\widetilde\chi_s$ are independent across $s$ and the increment at step $s$,
$\Delta_s=\1[i\in S_s](\1[\widetilde\chi_s=a]-\1[\widetilde\chi_s=b])$, has conditional mean zero (the seed assigns $a$ and
$b$ with equal probability $1/k$, so $\E[\1[\widetilde\chi_s=a]-\1[\widetilde\chi_s=b]\mid i\in S_s]=1/k-1/k=0$), this is a
martingale with increments $\Delta_s\in\{-1,0,1\}$. Its predictable quadratic variation is bounded by summing the per-step
conditional second moments. Each step contributes $\Delta_s^2=\1[i\in S_s]\1[\widetilde\chi_s\in\{a,b\}]$ (the cross term
vanishes since a color cannot equal both $a$ and $b$), and $i\in S_s$ and $\widetilde\chi_s\in\{a,b\}$ are independent, so
\[
  \langle\widetilde D\rangle_T\ =\ \sum_{s\le T}\E[\Delta_s^2]\ =\ \sum_{s\le T}\Pr(i\in S_s)\,\Pr(\widetilde\chi_s\in\{a,b\})
  \ =\ T\cdot\frac dn\cdot\frac2k\ =\ \frac{2Td}{nk}\ =:\ V,
\]
using that $i$ lies in a random $d$-sparse support with probability $d/n$ and that $\Pr(\widetilde\chi_s\in\{a,b\})=2/k$.
Freedman's inequality for a martingale with increments bounded by $1$ and predictable variation at most $V$ states
$\Pr(\max_t\widetilde D_{i,a,b}\ge u)\le\exp(-\tfrac{u^2/2}{V+u/3})$; taking the deviation
$u=32\Hscale$ gives
\[
  \Pr\big(\max_t\widetilde D_{i,a,b}\ge 32\Hscale\big)\ \le\ \exp\!\Big(-\frac{(32\Hscale)^2/2}{V+32\Hscale/3}\Big).
\]
The seed range at $i$ exceeds $32\Hscale$ only if some ordered pair of colors $(a,b)$ has $\widetilde D_{i,a,b}\ge 32\Hscale$,
so a union bound over the at most $k^2$ pairs gives
\[
  \Pr\big(\max_t\widetilde R_i\ge 32\Hscale\big)\ \le\ k^2\exp\!\Big(-\frac{512\Hscale^2}{V+\Oh(\Hscale)}\Big),
  \qquad 512\Hscale^2=\frac{(32\Hscale)^2}{2}.
\]
It remains to check that this bound clears $d^{-28}$ for the relevant $(k,d)$. The subtlety is that the Freedman denominator
is $V+\Oh(\Hscale)$, so the exponent does not grow without bound: with $T=\Th(n)$, $V=2Td/(nk)=\Th(d/k)$, the exponent is
$\Th(\Hscale^2/(V+\Hscale))$, which equals $\Th(\Hscale^2/V)=\Th(\Hscale^2 k/d)$ only while $V\gtrsim\Hscale$, and
\emph{saturates} at $\Th(\Hscale)$ once $V\lesssim\Hscale$. We split on this.

\emph{Regime $d/k\ge\Hscale$ (so $V\gtrsim\Hscale$).} The exponent is $\Th(\Hscale^2 k/d)$, growing linearly in $k$, while the
prefactor is only $k^2$; so $k^2\exp(-\Th(\Hscale^2 k/d))$ is decreasing in $k$ (log-derivative $2/k-\Th(\Hscale^2/d)<0$ since
$\Hscale^2/d\ge\log\Hscale\gg1$). Its maximum is at the smallest $k$, and at $k=2$, $d=\Hscale^2/\log\Hscale$ the exponent is
$\frac{512\Hscale^2}{V+\Oh(\Hscale)}=(512-o(1))\log\Hscale$, giving $\Hscale^{-512+o(1)}$. Since $d^{-28}=\Hscale^{-56+o(1)}$
here, this clears $d^{-28}$ with a factor-$9$ margin.

\emph{Regime $d/k<\Hscale$ (so $V\lesssim\Hscale$).} The exponent saturates at $\Th(512\Hscale^2/\Hscale)=\Th(\Hscale)$, so
the bound is $k^2 e^{-\Th(\Hscale)}$. For $k\le d$ this is $\le d^2 e^{-\Th(\Hscale)}=\Hscale^{\Oh(1)}(\log n)^{-\Th(1)}=o(d^{-28})$,
using $d^2\le\Hscale^{4+o(1)}$ and $e^{-\Th(\Hscale)}=(\log n)^{-\Th(1)}$. For a \emph{fixed} $k$ (any $d$), $k^2=\Oh(1)$ and
the same $e^{-\Th(\Hscale)}=(\log n)^{-\Th(1)}\le d^{-28}$ holds for large $n$ since $d^{-28}=\Om(1)$ when $d=\Oh(1)$.

So the seed tail is $\le d^{-28}$ for every $k\le d$ and every fixed $k$, which is the range of \Cref{lem:online-upper}. Thus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:seed-tail}
  \Pr(\max_t\widetilde R_i\ge 32\Hscale)\ \le\ d^{-28}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Parts 2--3: exceptional set and decay, by reduction to the two-color analysis.}
With the seed under control, the remaining work is to show that the exceptional coordinates stay sparse and that their ranges
cannot pile up much beyond $64\Hscale$. These are exactly the category and level-set analysis of \cite{AT2025}, and the key
point of this paper is that we do not have to redo them: they go through unchanged once two structural properties of the
repair rule are in place. We invoke them as a black box. Their analysis applies to any online algorithm built from a random
seed coloring together with a deterministic repair rule satisfying:
\begin{itemize}
  \item[(i)] the repair never raises the range of the coordinate it repairs;
  \item[(ii)] the assigned color differs from the seed only on arrivals whose support contains a coordinate already at range
  $\ge 64\Hscale$; and
  \item[(iii)] whenever an already-exceptional coordinate (range $\ge 64\Hscale$) has its range increased on an arrival, the
  support contains another coordinate whose range is at least as large.
\end{itemize}
Condition (iii) is the one that the pure-seed coloring (which trivially satisfies (i)--(ii) by never repairing) fails, and it
is exactly what the level-set recurrence needs: a coordinate climbing a high level must always exhibit a co-present helper at
least as high, which is what makes the level sets shrink. Under (i)--(iii) and the seed tail \eqref{eq:seed-tail},
\cite[exceptional-set lemma]{AT2025} gives $|E_T|\le n d^{-5}$ \whp,
and \cite[level-set lemma]{AT2025} gives, for $M(t,r)=\{i:\max_{s\le t}\rangeop_i(s)\ge 65\Hscale+3r\}$,
$|M(T,r)|\le n(\log n)^{-2\cdot 2^r}$. This bound shrinks doubly exponentially in $r$, so the level set is empty once
$r=\lceil 2\Hscale\rceil$, and at that point no coordinate has range $\ge 65\Hscale+3\cdot 2\Hscale$; in particular
$\max_{t,i}\rangeop_i<80\Hscale$. The reason both lemmas transfer is that they only ever use the independence of future
supports, the seed tail \eqref{eq:seed-tail}, and properties (i)--(iii); at no point do they look at how many colors there
are. The category construction in \cite{AT2025} indexes its depth by arrivals, not by colors, and the level-set recurrence
counts, for each coordinate that climbs a level, the co-present helper supplied by (iii). Both mechanisms are about which
arrivals touch which coordinates, so the color count enters nowhere.

It remains to check that our $k$-color algorithm satisfies (i)--(iii).

For (i): the repair acts only on an \emph{exceptional} coordinate $i^*\in E_{t-1}$, whose range is already
$\ge 64\Hscale\ge 1$, so its current range is strictly positive. The repair increments a minimum-load color $c^\star$ with
$x_{i^*,c^\star}(t-1)=\min_c x_{i^*,c}(t-1)=:\mathrm m$; write $\mathrm M=\max_c x_{i^*,c}(t-1)$, so $\mathrm M-\mathrm m\ge 1$
as the loads are integers and the range is positive. After incrementing, the load at $c^\star$ is $\mathrm m+1\le\mathrm M$,
so the maximum stays $\mathrm M$, while the minimum can only rise (it becomes the minimum of the remaining loads, each
$\ge\mathrm m$). Hence $\rangeop_{i^*}=\mathrm M-\min_c x_{i^*,c}$ does not increase. This holds for every $k$, and when
several colors tie for the minimum, incrementing any one of them moves a coordinate that was at the minimum, so the same
comparison applies. (Incrementing a minimum-load color of a coordinate of range $0$ would raise the range to $1$; the repair
never does this, because it fires only on exceptional coordinates, where the range is $\ge 64\Hscale$.)

For (ii): the assigned color equals the seed $\widetilde\chi_t$ unless the repair branch fires, and that branch fires only
when there is a (unique strict maximum) coordinate $i^*\in S_t\cap E_{t-1}$ to repair. In particular it fires only when
$S_t\cap E_{t-1}\ne\varnothing$, that is when the support $S_t$ contains a coordinate $i^*\in E_{t-1}$ already at range
$\ge 64\Hscale$ by the definition of the exceptional set $E_{t-1}$. So every arrival on which the color differs from the seed
has a co-present coordinate at range $\ge 64\Hscale$, which is exactly (ii).

For (iii): suppose an exceptional coordinate $j$ (range $\ge 64\Hscale$) has its range increased on arrival $t$. Then
$j\in S_t$ and $j$ was not the repaired coordinate, since the repair assigns a minimum-load color, which by (i) does not raise
the range; so $j$ took the seed. By definition the repair fires on the \emph{unique} coordinate of $S_t\cap E_{t-1}$ whose
range strictly exceeds that of every other coordinate of $S_t$. As $j\in S_t\cap E_{t-1}$ was not repaired, $j$ is not that
unique strict maximum, so some other coordinate $j'\in S_t$ has $\rangeop_{j'}\ge\rangeop_j$. Since
$\rangeop_{j'}\ge\rangeop_j\ge 64\Hscale$, this $j'$ is itself exceptional and is the co-present helper required by (iii).

All three properties are statements about the algorithm's control flow and the range function, with no dependence on the
number $k$ of colors. Hence the category and level-set analysis of \cite{AT2025} applies to our algorithm, and
$\max_{t,i}\rangeop_i<80\Hscale$ \whp. \qed

\begin{remark}
The reduction is the natural one: the only place the number of colors could matter is the repair, and a minimum-load repair
is range-non-increasing for every $k$. It is worth saying why the modified-incidence charging is insensitive to $k$. The
category recursion of \cite{AT2025} needs to pay for every place where the actual coloring deviates from the seed, and it
pays by charging each such deviation to a coordinate that was already exceptional and present in the same arrival. By
property (ii), every deviation comes with such a co-present exceptional coordinate, so the charge always has somewhere to
land. What the charging looks at is purely the binary event ``actual color $\ne$ seed''; it never asks which of the $k$
colors was actually used, so replacing two colors by $k$ colors leaves the count, and the recursion built on it, exactly the
same.
\end{remark}
