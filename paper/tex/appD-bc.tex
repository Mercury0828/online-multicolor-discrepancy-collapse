\section{The broadening-cost lemma and the phase conveyor}\label{app:bc}

This appendix supports the open problem of \Cref{sec:open}, and nothing in it feeds back into the main results: its
statements are not used by \Cref{thm:offline,thm:online,thm:separation}. The two objects we develop here are the adaptive
broadening-cost estimate, which is the positive input to the symmetric approach, and the deterministic phase conveyor, which
is the obstruction that rules out the expectation-level version of the asymmetric approach. Together they explain precisely
why the bracket of \Cref{sec:open} is hard to close from either side. We prove \Cref{lem:bc} in full, since it is a clean
probabilistic estimate; \Cref{prop:conveyor} is a deterministic construction whose verification we sketch, because it serves
only to delimit the open problem and not to support any theorem.

\paragraph{The broadening-cost lemma.}
We first set up the stopped process the estimate lives on. Fix a range level $r\ge2$ and a density $\eta\in(0,1)$. Let
$E_r(t)=\{i:\max_{u\le t}\rangeop_i(u)\ge r\}$ be the set of coordinates that have reached range at least $r$ by time $t$;
this set only grows in $t$. Stop the process the first time this set exceeds an $\eta$ fraction of all coordinates, at
$\sigma_{r,\eta}=\inf\{t:|E_r(t)|>\eta n\}$, so that up to the stopping time we always have $|E_r(t)|\le\eta n$. Let $B_{i,r}$
count the within-level broadenings of coordinate $i$ at range $r$, that is, the number of times $i$'s argmax set grows while
$i$ sits at range $r$, counted before the horizon $T\wedge\sigma_{r,\eta}$ and before $i$ first climbs to range $r+1$. The
lemma bounds the falling factorial moments $(B_{i,r})_a=B_{i,r}(B_{i,r}-1)\cdots(B_{i,r}-a+1)$, summed over coordinates, which
is the form a moment hierarchy consumes.

\begin{lemma}[Broadening cost]\label{lem:bc}
For every $r\ge2$ and integer $a\ge1$,
$\E\sum_{i=1}^n (B_{i,r})_a\le n\eta\,(2\tau_+ d^2\eta)^{a}$, uniformly over adaptive stopping-time epochs.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The argument is a charging argument followed by a sequential second-moment count. The point of the charging step is to show
that a broadening of $i$ can only happen when some \emph{other} coordinate that has already reached range $r$ is present in
the same support, so that broadenings are rare events tied to coincidences of two high-range coordinates.

Consider a broadening of $i$ at range $r$. By the definition of the rank, such a broadening reaches rank
$q=(k-1)(r-1)+\maxmult+1$. Because $r\ge2$, the coordinate $i$ has at least one color whose load is at most $\max-2$, and
this color lies outside the rank-$q$ forbidden set, so the rank-greedy rule would otherwise have used it. The cover
certificate of the rank-greedy rule then forces some other supported coordinate $j\ne i$ to cover $i$ at this step: the
broadening can only be the chosen move if a competing high-rank coordinate is present to block the cheaper alternatives. Any
coordinate of range at most $r-1$ has post-move rank at most $(k-1)(r-1)+1$, which is strictly less than $q$, so the covering
coordinate $j$ cannot have range below $r$; it must already lie in $E_r(t-1)$. Hence both $i$ and a distinct $j$ lie in
$S_t\cap E_r(t-1)$ at the time of the broadening. This is the charge: every broadening of $i$ is charged to the simultaneous
presence of $i$ and some already-range-$r$ coordinate $j$ in the support.

It follows that $B_{i,r}$ is dominated by the count $Y_i$ of arrival steps at which $i\in S_t$ and
$(S_t\setminus\{i\})\cap E_r(t-1)\ne\varnothing$, that is, steps where $i$ and at least one other range-$r$ coordinate share
the support. We now bound the factorial moments of $Y_i$. At any fixed step, the support $S_t$ is a uniform $d$-subset, so
the probability that $i\in S_t$ is $d/n$, and conditionally the probability that the remaining $d-1$ slots also meet the set
$E_r(t-1)$ of size $|E_r|\le\eta n$ is at most $d|E_r|/n$. The per-step conditional probability is therefore at most
$(d/n)\cdot(d|E_r|/n)\le 2d^2\eta/n=:p$. To bound $\E\sum_i(Y_i)_a$ we expand the falling factorial as a sum over ordered
$a$-tuples of distinct contributing steps and condition sequentially: at the first of the $a$ steps there are at most $\eta n$
eligible coordinates $i$ to anchor the tuple, and each subsequent step contributes a factor of at most $p$. Summing the
$\binom T a$ choices of step-set with the $a!$ orderings gives
$\E\sum_i(Y_i)_a\le a!\binom T a\,n\eta\,p^{a}\le n\eta\,(Tp)^{a}\le n\eta\,(2\tau_+ d^2\eta)^{a}$, where in the last step we
used $T\le\tau_+ n$ to write $Tp\le 2\tau_+ d^2\eta$. Because the supports at future steps remain i.i.d.\ uniform $d$-sets
regardless of the past, the per-step bound $p$ does not depend on the history, so the whole estimate is uniform over the
adaptive stopping-time epoch, as claimed.
\end{proof}

It is worth seeing how this clean estimate nonetheless produces the $\log k$ gap. Combined with the level-raising cover
bound, which says a coordinate reaches range $r+1$ only after accumulating enough range-$r$ broadenings, \Cref{lem:bc} feeds
into a symmetric range recurrence. The factorial-moment bound $n\eta(2\tau_+d^2\eta)^a$ behaves like the moments of a Poisson
variable of mean $\Th(d^2\eta)$, and demanding $k$ such broadenings to force a raise turns this into
$\eta_{r+1}\lesssim(C\eta_r)^k$ for the fraction $\eta_r$ at range $r$. Iterating gives $\eta\le e^{-ck^{j}}$ after $j$
levels, so making this beat a union bound over the $n$ coordinates requires $k^{j}\gtrsim\log n$, that is
$j\gtrsim\loglog n/\log k$, while the bracket only allows $j$ up to $\loglog n/k$. The discrepancy is the $\log k$ gap of
\Cref{sec:open}. As explained there, the gap is intrinsic to any permutation-invariant statistic, since the skip
configuration forces the full $k^{r}$ branching that the symmetric recurrence records; the estimate above is tight, and the
loss is in the symmetry of the statistic and not in the bound.

\paragraph{The phase conveyor.}
We turn to the asymmetric route and the construction that defeats its expectation-level form. The asymmetric rule we analyze
is \emph{LeftGap}, the color-sensitive analogue of Always-Go-Left. For a coordinate $i$ and color $c$ write
$g_{i,c}=x_{i,c}-\min_b x_{i,b}$ for the gap of color $c$ above the minimum load on $i$, and let $h_c=\max_{i\in S_t}g_{i,c}$
be the largest such gap over the current support. LeftGap picks the color $c$ that minimizes the pair $(h_c,c)$
lexicographically, so it prefers colors with small maximum gap and breaks ties toward the lowest color index, the
``leftmost'' direction. To track how gaps propagate we encode the event $g_{i,c}\ge\ell$ as a token of phase
$k(\ell-1)+c$, so that phases increase first through the color index $c$ within a fixed gap level $\ell$ and then jump to the
next level. In a typical instance the number of coordinates reaching a high phase obeys the staggered-Fibonacci recurrence
$F_k$, and the conjectured bound would follow from an expectation-level tail on the number of high-phase tokens. The
proposition shows that no such expectation-level tail can hold.

\begin{proposition}[No expectation-level tail for LeftGap]\label{prop:conveyor}
For $k=d\ge3$ and every $q\ge1$ there is a deterministic sequence of $q$ exactly-$k$-sparse supports on which LeftGap creates
a principal token of each phase $1,2,\dots,q$ on the successive arrivals, using $\Oh(kq)$ coordinates. Consequently, if
$Z_u$ counts principal LeftGap tokens of phase $\ge u$, then $\E Z_u\ge\binom nk^{-\lceil u\rceil}$, which for
$k=\lfloor\Hscale^2/(2\log\Hscale)\rfloor$ exceeds $e^{-cF_k(m)}$ at $u=kB+m$ for any fixed $m>\Hscale/\log2$. Hence no bound
$\E Z_{kB+m}\le Cnd\,e^{-cF_k(m)}$ holds on a window reaching $m=\Th(\Hscale)$.
\end{proposition}

The mechanism behind the proposition is a cycle invariant that lets a fixed set of coordinates be reused indefinitely. The
conveyor keeps three core coordinates alive, an anchor $a$ and two carriers $b_1$ and $b_j$, and feeds in fresh filler
coordinates each cycle. After $r$ completed cycles it maintains the gap profiles $g_a=(0,r,\dots,r)$,
$g_{b_1}=(r,0,\dots,0)$, and $g_{b_j}=r(e_1+e_j)$. These profiles are arranged so that LeftGap, following its leftmost
tie-breaking, is forced to advance exactly one of the carriers each cycle while restoring the others, and one more cycle
advances the maximum phase by exactly $k$. The advance is therefore linear in the number of cycles and not Fibonacci: the
conveyor manufactures one full level of $k$ phases per cycle using only the recycled cores plus $\Oh(k)$ fillers, instead of
the exponentially many coordinates a typical instance would spend. This is exactly why it inflates the expectation past the
$e^{-cF_k(m)}$ that the asymmetric approach needs.

The construction is, however, vanishingly rare. Realizing the prescribed sequence of $q$ exactly-$k$-sparse supports has
probability $\binom nk^{-q}=\exp(-(\log n)\Hscale^{\Oh(1)})$, which is smaller than $n^{-A}$ for every fixed $A$. So the
conveyor does not contradict any high-probability bound, and it is not an online lower bound; what it refutes is the
expectation-level tail, and with it the stopping-time-uniform mixed-profile factorization that an expectation argument would
rely on. This pins down what a correct argument must look like. The corrected, sufficient statement for \Cref{prob:growing}
is the truncated bound $\Pr(Z_{kB+m}>0)\le Cnd\,e^{-cF_k(m)}+n^{-A}$, which adds an explicit $n^{-A}$ slack to absorb the rare
conveyor and so is consistent with both the conveyor and the conjecture. Proving it would require a cycle-aware
``fold-or-Fibonacci'' count: an accounting that, every time a coordinate is reused, charges the reuse to enough genuinely
independent earlier births that the recycled conveyor cannot be assembled cheaply. Producing such a count is exactly the
content of the open problem, and it remains open.
